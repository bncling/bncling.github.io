Internal direct products

Definition Let \(G\) be a group with subgroups \(H\) and \(K\). We say that \(G\) is the internal direct product of \(H\) and \(K\) if and only if the follow three conditions hold: \(H\) and \(K\) are normal in \(G\) (\(H, K \triangleleft G\)), \(H\) and \(K\) intersect trivially (\(H \cap K = \{e\}\)), \(G\) is the set \(HK = \{hk \mid h \in H, k \in K\}\). Lagrange's theorem

Statement Let \((G, \cdot)\) be a finite group and let \(H\) be a subgroup of \(G\). Then \(\vert G \vert = [G:H] \vert H \vert\), and therefore the order of \(H\) divides the order of \(G\). Order of an element in a group

Definition Let \((G, \cdot)\) be a group and let \(g \in G\). The order of \(g\), denoted \(\vert g \vert\) is the smallest positive integer \(n\) such that \(g^n = e\), the identity element of \(G\). If such an \(n\) exists, \(g\) has finite order. Otherwise, \(g\) has infinite order. Properties By Lagrange’s Theorem, the order of \(g\) always divides the order of the group, \(\vert G \vert\). The alternating group

Definition The alternating group, denoted \(A_n\), is the normal subgroup of \(S_n\) containing all even permutations. Properties The group has order \(n! / 2\). It is not abelian or cyclic for \(n \geq 4\), and it is simple for \(n \geq 5\). Because \([S_n:A_n] = 2\), \(A_n\) is normal in \(S_n\). Examples The group \(A_4\) has order \(12\) but has no subgroups of order \(6\). This is because if \(H\) is a subgroup of order \(6\), it contains at least two \(3\)-cycles because \(A_4\) contains all eight \(3\)-cycles (since all odd-length cycles are even)....
