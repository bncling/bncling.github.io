Group homomorphisms

Definitions Let \((G, \cdot)\) and \((H, \circ)\) be groups. A map \(\varphi: G \to H\) is called a group homomorphism if it preserves the group operation. That is, \(\varphi\) is a homomorphism if and only if for all \(a,b \in G\) we have \(\varphi(a \cdot b) = \varphi(a) \circ \varphi(b)\). Internal direct products

Definition Let \(G\) be a group with subgroups \(H\) and \(K\). We say that \(G\) is the internal direct product of \(H\) and \(K\) if and only if the follow three conditions hold: \(H\) and \(K\) are normal in \(G\) (\(H, K \triangleleft G\)), \(H\) and \(K\) intersect trivially (\(H \cap K = \{e\}\)), \(G\) is the set \(HK = \{hk \mid h \in H, k \in K\}\). Order of an element in a group

Definition Let \((G, \cdot)\) be a group and let \(g \in G\). The order of \(g\), denoted \(\vert g \vert\) is the smallest positive integer \(n\) such that \(g^n = e\), the identity element of \(G\). If such an \(n\) exists, \(g\) has finite order. Otherwise, \(g\) has infinite order. Properties By Lagrange’s Theorem, the order of \(g\) always divides the order of the group, \(\vert G \vert\).
