Homeomorphisms

February 9, 2022

Definition

Let \(X\) and \(Y\) be topological spaces. A function \(f: X \to Y\) is called a homeomorphism if it is bijective and both \(f\) and \(f\inv\) are continuous. When there exists a homeomorphism \(f: X \to Y\), we say the two spaces \(X\) and \(Y\) are homeomorphic or that they are topologically equivalent.

Properties

Theorem 7.26: Being homeomorphic is an equivalence relation on topological spaces.

Theorem 7.28: If \(f: X \to Y\) is a continuous bijection, then being open or closed is equivalent to being a homeomorphism.

Theorem 7.29: If \(X\) is a compact space and \(Y\) is Hausdorff, then all continuous bijections \(f: X \to Y\) are homeomorphisms.

Corollary 7.31: Let \(X\) be compact and \(Y\) be Hausdorff. Then if \(f: X \to Y\) is an injective and continuous map, it is an embedding.

Examples

If \(a < b\), then the interval \((a,b)\) as a subspace of \(\Rs\) (\(\R\) with the standard topology) is homeomorphic to \(\Rs\).

In the \(X \times Y\) with the product topology, the slice \(X \times \{y\}\) as a subspace of \(X \times Y\) is homeomorphic to the space \(X\).
