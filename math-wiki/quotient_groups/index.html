Quotient groups

December 22, 2021

Also called factor groups.

Definition

Let \((G, \cdot)\) be a group with normal subgroup \(N\). Then its corresponding quotient group, denoted \(G/N\), is the set of all left cosets of \(G\) (though \(N \triangleleft G\) means these are the same as the right cosets) together with group operation \(\cdot: G/N \times G/N\) given as follows. If \(aN, bN \in G/N\), then \(aN \cdot bN = (ab) N\).

Properties

The quotient group is a group. This requires checking that the group operation is well-defined, which can be seen as follows. If \(aN = a'N\) and \(bN = b'N\), then we have that \(a\inv a' \in N\), which means there exists an \(n_1 \in N\) such that \(a\inv a' = n_1\). Since \(N\) is normal, we have that \(b\inv n_1 \in b\inv N = N b\inv\), meaning there exists an \(n_2 \in N\) such that \(b\inv n_1 = n_2 b\inv\). Therefore we have that \[(ab)\inv (a'b') = b\inv a\inv a'b' = b\inv n_1 b' = n_2 b\inv b',\] and we know that \(b\inv b' \in N\) because \(bN = b'N\). Therefore \((ab)\inv (a'b') = n_2 b\inv b' \in N\), which means \((ab)N = (a'b')N\) and so the group operation on the quotient group is indeed well-defined.

Another Way to Think About This

Since every normal subgroup \(N\) is the kernel of some homomorphism \(\varphi: G \to G'\), we look at the image of \(\varphi\) and note that for each element in the image, its fiber is a coset of \(G\). Since the image of \(\varphi\) is a subgroup of \(G'\), we have a bijective association between cosets and elements in this subgroup of the image, meaning we can easily put a group structure on the set of cosets \(G/N\). (See the first isomorphism theorem.)

Examples

The symmetric group \(S_n\) has normal subgroup \(A_n\), the alternating group, and its quotient is isomorphic to \(\Z_2\).

For any group \(G\), its center, the subgroup \(Z(G)\), is normal in \(G\), and the the quotient group is \(G/Z(G) \cong \inn(G)\), the inner automorphism group.

Since the group of integers under addition is abelian, the subgroup \(n\Z\) is normal in \(\Z\), and the quotient group is \(\Z / n\Z \cong \Z_n\).
