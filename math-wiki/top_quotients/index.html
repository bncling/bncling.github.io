Quotient spaces

March 2, 2022

Generalizing identification spaces.

Definitions

Let \((X,\T)\) be a topological spaces, let \(Y\) be a set, and let \(f: X \to Y\) be a surjection. Define the quotient topology on \(Y\) to be the topology in which \(U \subset Y\) is open if and only if \(f\inv(U)\) is open in \(X\).

Given a surjective map \(f: X \to Y\) between topological spaces \(X\) and \(Y\), we call \(f\) a quotient map and \(Y\) a quotient space if and only if the topology on \(Y\) is the quotient topology with respect to \(Y\).

Properties

Theorem 7.48: The quotient topology on \(Y\) with respect to a surjection \(f: X \to Y\) is the finest topology on \(Y\) such that the map \(f\) is continuous.

Theorems 7.49 and 7.50: If \(f: X \to Y\) is a continuous surjection between topological spaces \(X\) and \(Y\), \(f\) being an open map implies that \(f\) is a quotient map, and \(f\) being a closed map implies that \(f\) is a quotient map.

Theorem 7.53: If \(f: X \to Y\) is a quotient map, then a map \(g: Y \to Z\) out of the quotient space \(Y\) is continuous if and only of \(g \circ f: X \to Z\) is continuous.

Examples

Identification maps are quotient maps, as are projection maps.
